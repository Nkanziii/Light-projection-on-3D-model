<h2>Project Concept</h2>
<p>
  I often draw inspiration from my own life when creating work. My practice is rooted in personal experience, memory, and identity. I am drawn to creating pieces that are deeply connected to who I am, work that allows me to explore my background and the circumstances that have shaped me.

As a woman from Iran, where I was born and spent my early childhood, I have always been aware of the striking contrast between life in the Middle East and life in the West. Narratives surrounding Muslim women and oppression are often presented as simple or one-dimensional, shaped by media portrayals and political discourse. Yet the reality is far more complex. There is a significant difference between what is written in the Qur’an, what is interpreted through culture and power structures, and what is practiced in many societies across the Middle East.

Growing up in Iran, religion was not simply a matter of personal belief—it was embedded into everyday life through enforcement and expectation. Being covered, being quiet, and learning not to question authority were treated as ordinary parts of growing up. Speaking openly about injustice was rarely encouraged, and leaving such an environment was not easy.

Despite this, I want to acknowledge that my childhood was still filled with warmth and love. The people around me—family, neighbours, and friends—did not represent the system that governed them. What many of us shared, however, was the quiet understanding that freedom and opportunity often felt distant.

Moving to the United Kingdom revealed an entirely different world. I heard people speak openly about freedom of speech, but at first I struggled to fully understand what that meant. I remember thinking: how can someone openly criticise their government and face no punishment? That idea alone felt unfamiliar. Over time, I began to appreciate the space that such freedoms create.

As I grew older, I began questioning religion—particularly Islam. In the society I came from, questioning faith was often condemned. Yet when I began to explore the religion more deeply myself, I was surprised to discover that seeking knowledge and asking questions were not discouraged within the faith. In fact, reflection and understanding were central to it.

I also began to notice a profound gap between the treatment of women in many Muslim-majority societies and the principles outlined in the Qur’an. In practice, women are often silenced, confined to domestic roles, and discouraged from challenging male authority. This never aligned with my own values as someone who believes strongly in justice and equality.

What I found within the text itself, however, suggested something very different. The Qur’an introduced rights and dignity for women within a historical context that had previously denied them both. Its intentions were not to silence women but to liberate them from systems of oppression. Yet, as history has shown, religion can be manipulated and used as a tool of control.

Through my creative work, I want to challenge stereotypes imposed on women, particularly within my own experiences. I want to encourage people to think critically and to recognise that these conversations are never simply black and white. They are layered, nuanced, and deeply human.

It is possible to hold multiple truths at once. I can advocate for women’s rights, speak against religious oppression in any form, and still be spiritually aligned with Islam. I believe that questioning injustice, seeking understanding, and speaking openly are not acts of betrayal—they are acts of integrity.

In a world where people are often encouraged to choose rigid sides, I hope my work invites reflection instead of division, and curiosity instead of certainty.

This project asks:

<ul>
  <li>Who defines religious truth?</li>
  <li>Who benefits from its interpretation?</li>
  <li>What happens when women’s identities are shaped by systems built without them?</li>
  <li>What does liberation look like when space itself becomes restricted?</li>
</ul>

I wanted to explore this project through a new medium and explore three.js as my creative tool, I wanted to create a 3D model of a veiled woman, within a dark background and light rotating around the figure, the dark atmosphere is supposed to give a confinded and imprisoned illusion to the figure, like it is bound and oppessed, with arabic scripture around her, the idea that she is imprisoned by the very thing that is supposed to give her rights and freedom.

Exploring scripture as light. But light, when controlled, becomes shadow.


</p>
